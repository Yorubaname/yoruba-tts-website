    What is TTS?

    This is an abbreviation for Text-to-Speech, a program designed to convert text input (words, phrases, sentences) into audio. It is an application that can pronounce Yorùbá words, phrases, sentences, from text input.

    Why TTSYoruba?

    Yorùbá is a language spoken by over 30 million people in Nigeria alone and by millions more in Benin Republic, Togo, Ghana, Brazil, Cuba, Jamaica, and the United States. It is also a language with an extensive and enduring literary and religious value, but with inadequate representation in technology (like most African languages). By creating tools like the TTS machine (and many more like the YorubaName dictionary, free downloadable keyboards, etc, we are trying to better empower African languages to serve interested users in the technological age.

    How can I use this tool?

    It can be used as either a text reader or tone/spelling verifier or as a tone marking teacher.

    As a text reader, you can paste a body of text in the space provided and have the machine read it to you. This will work for people who have problems reading Yorùbá texts but can comprehend it if spoken to them.

    As tone/spelling verifier, you can use TTSYorùbá to verify that the tonemarking you have placed on a piece of text is correct by having the machine pronounce it for you first. This also works for people who have had clients deliver translated Yorùbá texts to them. By copying and pasting random segments of the delivered work into this machine, you can hear the output and know whether the work is well done or not.

    As a tone marking teacher, you will be able to use the application to actually understand how different tones are pronounced. By the time you try out different tonal combinations on different words, you will eventually figure out how tonemarking works in Yorùbá. So, in some way, this can actually teach you how to learn to write properly in Yorùbá.

    How else can I use it?

    You can also use it to send audio missives online. For instance, each generated sound can be shared on Twitter or Facebook, or even copied and sent through messenger or other means. That way, texting becomes not the only way to communicate. You can now send sounds, which can be intimate or mischievous, as you want it. Up to you how you decide to use it.

    Why Yoruba?

    This is an experiment in finding out whether speech synthesis is possible in an African language. We wrote about the thought process behind this decision here. Yorùbá is the natural first choice because the TTS machine was created to support a Yorùbá Names Dictionary at YorubaName.com. We are open to trying out for more languages provided we get support. If you are a linguist or developer interested in collaborating with us on this, do send us an email.

    The audio output isn’t correct. What gives?

    Check your tone marking. The tool was built with the understanding of Yorùbá phonology. So what you put in is what you get out.

    I can’t tone mark my input because I don’t have a keyboard that can put the tone marks.

    Well, you’re in luck. You don’t need a physical keyboard anymore. We created a free tonemarking software which you can download here.

    Okay, I’m a millennial and don’t want to learn the tonemarking skill at all. I just want to type in a bland text and get a good output. This is technology after all.

    Lol, you’re right, and we empathize. We will say you should rethink your stance. We will however like to make you happy by letting you know that we are currently working towards a more intuitive app that can understand your text input from context and tonemark for you appropriately. But before then, do give this a try. You might actually find it very easy to learn.

    I am building a tech product and I’ll like to use your Text-to-speech API so my product can “speak” Yorùbá to our customers. How do I do that?

    Send us an email at project@yorubaname.com with “TTS inquiry” in the subject line. If your product is open source and not commercial, you might be able to use our work for free.

    Are you looking to create TTS in other African languages?

    Yes, as we said above. Even this work, as it is, isn’t complete. We are working on further iterations to make the output more natural sounding. Under the banner of Orature Lab, a research and advocacy initiative for African languages, we are working to create new opportunities for African languages to thrive by empowering them with tech and documentation initiatives.
